Before Vivid existed, our formulation team spent years reading other people's labels. Here's the sixty-second check we run on any bottle — ours included. Bring this list to any health shop in the country.

## 1. Is every ingredient dosed in milligrams?

The single biggest tell. If a label says "proprietary blend — 450mg" across six ingredients, you cannot know whether the headline herb is dosed at something meaningful or at a pinch. Blends hide ratios; ratios are the product. We publish milligrams per capsule for everything we make, which is why our [comparison page](/pages/compare-vivid) exists.

## 2. Is the form named?

"Magnesium 400mg" is not information — oxide, citrate and glycinate behave differently. "Vitamin C" could be ascorbic acid or a buffered ascorbate. "Turmeric" could be ground root (mostly fibre) or a standardised extract. Our turmeric label tells you which and how much; a label that won't name the form is making a decision for you and hoping you don't ask.

## 3. Does the dose match the studies?

This one takes a phone and thirty seconds. If curcumin trials used 500–1,000mg standardised extract and the capsule holds 50mg of ground root, the bottle is a souvenir. We publish our reasoning per ingredient on the [ingredient pages](/pages/ingredient-standards) — and where our dose sits below a studied dose for a legitimate reason (tolerability, stacking), the label says so.

## 4. Who made it, and where?

"Distributed by" is not "manufactured by". Look for the actual manufacturing statement. Ours is made in Centurion in a SAHPRA-licensed facility, and [our sourcing page](/pages/our-sourcing) names supplier and country per key ingredient — the test is whether a brand will put names in writing.

## 5. What's the capsule made of?

Vegetable capsule (HPMC) or gelatine? Any colourants, titanium dioxide, unnecessary fillers? Short excipient lists are a quiet mark of care. MSM is a good test case: it's a single compound, so the label should be almost empty — [ours is](/products/vivid-health-vivid-body-msm-90-capsules).

## 6. Does anything on the label promise to treat a disease?

South African law is clear: supplements support normal function; they don't treat, cure or prevent disease. A label (or salesperson) promising to cure your arthritis is breaking the rule and telling you everything about the brand's honesty in one sentence. Supports, assists, maintains — that's the legal and honest vocabulary.

## The honest bit

A perfect label doesn't guarantee the product works for you — biology is individual, and even well-dosed, well-formed ingredients help some people more than others. What a transparent label guarantees is that you can make a real decision, and that the brand expects to be checked. That expectation changes how a company behaves long before you swallow anything.
