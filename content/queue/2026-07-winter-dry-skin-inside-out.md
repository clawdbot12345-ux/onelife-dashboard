A customer pulled up her sleeve at the counter last week to show me the white scratch lines on her forearm — "Look, I can write my name on myself." Every July it's the same parade: itchy shins, tight cheeks after washing, lips that crack by lunchtime. And almost everyone is fighting it with cream alone.

Cream matters. But after thirty years of Highveld winters, I can tell you the people who win this fight work from both sides of the skin.

## What July actually does to your skin

Your skin's outer layer is a brick wall — skin cells are the bricks, and the mortar is made of fats your body builds itself. That mortar is what holds water in. Winter attacks it three ways at once. Highveld air is at its driest, so moisture gets pulled out of your skin faster than usual. Then we make it worse indoors: heaters dry the air further, and the long scalding shower that feels so good on a cold morning strips the mortar like dishwashing liquid strips a greasy pan.

Meanwhile the inside supply line slows down. The fats your skin uses for that mortar come from your diet — and a winter of toast, stews and very little oily fish doesn't deliver many of them. Collagen, the protein scaffolding under the surface, is also being rebuilt more slowly every year after about thirty. Dry air outside, fewer building materials inside: that's heater skin.

## What we'd actually suggest

**Omega-3, daily, with food.** These are the fats the skin barrier is genuinely built from, and the ones most winter diets run short of. One to two softgels a day with your biggest meal. If you remember nothing else from this article: supplements that support your skin are eaten with food and judged after eight weeks, not eight days.

**Collagen, one honest scoop.** Around 10g of hydrolysed collagen a day, dissolved in coffee or porridge — it's tasteless, which is exactly why people actually keep the habit. The research on skin hydration and elasticity used daily doses over eight to twelve weeks, so buy it planning to finish the tub, or don't buy it at all.

**Vitamin E as the bodyguard.** It's the antioxidant your body stations in fatty tissue — including that skin mortar — to protect it. One capsule a day alongside the omega-3 makes a sensible pair. Don't megadose it; more is not better here.

And the free advice that outperforms all three: shorter, cooler showers (warm, not braai-coal hot), moisturiser applied on still-damp skin within three minutes of towelling off, and a bowl of water near the heater if the air in your bedroom crackles with static by morning. Don't forget to actually drink water, either — the flask of rooibos and three coffees that carry most of us through a Joburg winter day are not the same thing as hydration, and skin is the last organ in the queue when supplies run short.

One more counter observation: hands and lips fail first because they have the thinnest barrier and do the most washing. If your knuckles are cracking, the fix is the same stack plus a tube of hand cream at every basin you use — kitchen, bathroom, office. Make it impossible to forget rather than trying to remember.

## When NOT to bother

If you're keeping the twenty-minute scalding shower, save your money — no capsule can out-supply what that strips off every morning. Fix the shower first; it's free. If you already eat oily fish like sardines or salmon twice a week, your omega-3 supply is probably fine. And if your skin is cracked, bleeding, weeping or fiercely itchy in patches, that's not winter dryness — that could be eczema or something else that needs a doctor's eyes, not our shelves.
