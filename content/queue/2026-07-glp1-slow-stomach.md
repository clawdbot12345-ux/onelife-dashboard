There's a particular look people get in week three on a GLP-1. A young woman wore it at our Glen Village counter recently: hand resting on her stomach, that queasy 1,000-yard stare. "Precious, I love what the scale says. But I feel like I swallowed a brick and it's still... *there*." The brick is not your imagination. It's the mechanism.

## Why the traffic jam is the medicine working

GLP-1 medications slow gastric emptying - food leaves your stomach in the slow lane on purpose, which is a big part of why you feel full on tiny portions. But the same slow lane explains most of the side-effect bingo card: nausea (a full stomach signals "no more, thanks" loudly), bloating (food sitting longer means more fermentation), reflux for some, and constipation downstream - less food and less fluid means less to move, moving slower.

Knowing it's mechanical matters, because the fixes are mostly mechanical too.

## What we'd actually suggest

**Eat like the stomach you have now.** Small plates, eaten slowly, stopping at the *first* full signal - not the third. Low-fat over fried (fat slows emptying even further). Cold or room-temperature food often sits better than hot on queasy days. This free advice does more than anything in a bottle.

**Ginger and peppermint for the queasiness.** Boring, old-fashioned, and among the few botanicals with genuinely respectable evidence for nausea. Ginger tea counts - a thumb of fresh root steeped ten minutes, sipped warm-not-hot. The Viridian capsules bundle both with digestive enzymes if you'd rather have one thing to take. One caution from the counter: peppermint can loosen the valve at the top of the stomach, so if reflux is your bigger battle, lead with ginger alone and see how you respond before adding mint to the mix.

**Enzyme support for the brick meals.** When a proper dinner is unavoidable - Sunday lunch, a braai - a digestive enzyme with the meal helps break it down while it waits in the queue. It's support, not a licence to eat a mountain.

**Water, salt, movement for the slow lane downstream.** Constipation on a GLP-1 is nearly universal and nearly always improved by the unglamorous trio: more water (add an electrolyte stick - you're likely under-drinking too), enough fibre *gradually*, and a 20-minute walk after your biggest meal. If that fails, psyllium is the gentle next step - ask us in store.

## Reflux, and surviving a braai

Two situations deserve their own paragraph. First, reflux: a slower stomach means more time for contents to press upward, and some people meet heartburn for the first time on a GLP-1. The mechanical fixes come first - nothing within three hours of lying down, smaller suppers, left side if you're a side sleeper, and lift the head of the bed a brick's worth if nights are bad. Persistent or painful reflux is prescriber territory; there are proper options, and white-knuckling it isn't one.

Second, the braai problem. South African social life is built around exactly the meal a GLP-1 stomach handles worst: fatty, smoky, generous, late. You don't have to become a hermit. Arrive not-starving, load the plate you *intend* to finish (a side plate works wonders psychologically), lead with the salads and lean cuts, take an enzyme with the first bite, and let the boerewors be a taste rather than a mission. Nobody at the fire is auditing your plate - and the version of you that isn't green at 9pm is better company anyway.

## When NOT to bother

If your nausea is mild and fading week by week, you don't need products - you need patience and smaller forks. Save your money. And know the red lines: repeated vomiting, inability to keep fluids down, severe or persistent abdominal pain, or signs of dehydration are **phone-your-doctor-today** symptoms, possibly dose-adjustment territory. No supplement on our shelves is the answer to those, and anyone who tells you otherwise is dangerous. As always - your prescriber should know every capsule you take alongside the injection.
