Every July it happens at least once a day: someone shuffles in wrapped in a scarf, leans on the counter and croaks, *"Precious, give me the strongest thing you've got."* And every July I give the same disappointing, honest answer: by the time you sound like that, most of what we sell has missed its window.

But not everything. Here's the flu-week truth, from thirty years of winters at this counter.

## What's actually happening in there

A cold or flu is a virus running a course, and the thing fighting it is your own immune system — not anything in a bottle. Nothing on our shelves kills the virus. What supplements can honestly do is *support* the worker: give your immune cells the minerals they're firing through, help your airways clear the mess, and look after the parts of you that take collateral damage along the way. Anyone promising more than that is selling you the label.

That reframe changes what's worth buying. You're not shopping for a cure. You're shopping for a slightly shorter, slightly more comfortable week — and mostly, you're shopping for rest, fluids and patience.

## What we'd actually hand you

**Zinc lozenges — but only if you start immediately.** This is the one with a genuine early window. The studies that found shorter colds started zinc within the first 24 hours of symptoms and used lozenges dissolved slowly in the mouth, every few hours, not swallowed like a tablet. So the honest instruction: keep a tube in the bathroom cabinet *now*, in July, and start at the first throat-scratch — one lozenge every two to three waking hours for the first few days. Starting on day four is feeding the horse after it's bolted. Don't run high-dose zinc for weeks on end either; it's a sprint mineral, not a marathon one.

**NAC for the brick in your chest.** N-acetyl cysteine supports thinning thick mucus, which is why the second half of a cold — the part where you sound like a tuba — is where it earns its place. It also feeds your body's production of glutathione, its own in-house antioxidant, which gets spent fast while you're fighting something. One capsule two to three times a day with water, plenty of water.

**The unglamorous champions: rest, fluids, soup.** Your immune system does its heaviest work while you sleep. The person who takes two days of proper rest usually beats the person who "soldiers on" with a pocket full of capsules — and then shares the prize with the whole office. There is no supplement version of staying in bed. Warm fluids count double in a dry Highveld winter: a raw throat and bricked-up sinuses are partly a moisture problem, and soup, rooibos and honey-lemon water do quiet mechanical work that no capsule replaces.

**And if antibiotics enter the picture** — only if your doctor prescribed them, because antibiotics do nothing to a virus — they'll flatten your gut flora along with the bacteria they're aimed at. A short course of an acute-care probiotic, taken a few hours apart from the antibiotic and continued a few days after, supports putting that population back on its feet.

## When NOT to bother — and when to go to a doctor instead

If you're on day five and mostly mending, save your money: you've done the hard part, and a basket of immune products now is a donation. The prevention stack (vitamin D, echinacea and friends) is a *next winter* conversation, started in May — not a mid-flu purchase.

More importantly: a fever above 39° that lasts more than three days, chest pain, shortness of breath, confusion, or flu in someone pregnant, elderly or living with a chronic condition — that's a doctor, today, not a lozenge. We will always rather lose the sale than have you standing at our counter when you should be in a consulting room.
