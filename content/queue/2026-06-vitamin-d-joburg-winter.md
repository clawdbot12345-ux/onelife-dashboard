"But we live in South Africa!" — the most common thing said at our counter when a customer's doctor has just told them their vitamin D is low. Sunny SA, low sunshine vitamin. It sounds wrong. It's also one of the most ordinary findings in the country every winter.

## How sunny SA runs short of the sunshine vitamin

Your skin makes vitamin D from a specific slice of sunlight — and in a Highveld winter, three things quietly cut you off from it. The winter sun sits low, and that slice of light weakens. The cold keeps you covered: long sleeves, closed shoes, ten brisk steps from the car to the office door. And those office hours mean the only generous sun of the day happens while you're under fluorescent lighting.

Track an ordinary winter weekday and the skin-on-direct-sun count is often close to zero. Repeat for ninety days of winter and stores genuinely run down — in Joburg as surely as in London, just for different reasons.

## Why you should care (it's not just bones)

Vitamin D's old reputation is bones and calcium, and that part is true. But the reason we talk about it every winter is its role in **immune function** — your defence cells carry receptors for it and work better when it's available. It also turns up consistently in conversations about winter mood and energy: the flat, heavy, "I'm fine but I'm not" feeling that arrives with the short days. We'd never call a supplement a cure for that — but topping up a vitamin you're genuinely low on is the cheapest first move there is.

## What we actually suggest

A daily vitamin D3 through winter — D3 specifically, the form your skin would have made. For most adults a steady daily 1,000 IU through June, July and August is a sensible maintenance floor; people who test properly low often need more for a while, which is a conversation for your doctor and a blood test, not a guess at our counter.

The habit matters more than the heroics, which is why we rate the **oral spray**: under the tongue, three seconds, no pill to swallow, lives next to your toothbrush. The best supplement is the one you actually take sixty days in a row. D is fat-soluble, so if you take capsules instead, take them with your biggest meal.

## When NOT to bother

If you work outdoors, garden on weekends in short sleeves, or you're the parent on touchline duty every Saturday morning — your winter sun exposure may genuinely be fine, and we'll happily tell you to keep your R200. And vitamin D is the one supplement where *more* is not better long-term: don't stack three different high-dose D products on top of each other.
