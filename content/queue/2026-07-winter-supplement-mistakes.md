We sell supplements for a living, so this article is slightly against our own interests. But after thirty years behind the counter, the thing that actually keeps people coming back isn't what we sell them — it's what we talk them out of. So here it is: the five ways we watch good money go to waste every single winter.

## 1. Buying the cure after the cold has arrived

Half of winter's immune spending happens in the 48 hours *after* the throat starts burning. By then, most preventatives have missed their window. Echinacea is the textbook case — the research that made its name is about taking it through the season, before exposure. There are things worth taking once you're sick, but the panicked Tuesday-morning basket of six products is mostly a donation. The honest move: build the small daily stack in June, not the emergency one in July.

## 2. Paying for twenty ingredients at doses that do nothing

Turn over the "ultimate immune complex" and read the amounts. Twenty impressive names, each at a tenth of a meaningful dose — a label written for marketing, not for you. Three single products at honest doses beat one shotgun blend nearly every time. It's why our protocols are short.

## 3. Taking the same thing three times without knowing it

The multivitamin has zinc. The immune blend has zinc. The new lozenges? Also zinc. Nobody decided to triple-dose — it just accumulated, and past a point extra zinc upsets your stomach and competes with other minerals. This is the most common thing we catch when someone empties their supplement drawer onto our counter. Do that — or WhatsApp us photos of your labels — before you buy anything new this winter. The check is free, and "you're already covered" is an answer we give daily.

## 4. Buying the cheap form, feeling nothing, deciding it "doesn't work"

The saddest waste isn't a bad product — it's the right ingredient in the wrong form. Magnesium oxide that mostly visits the bathroom. Vitamin C that never gets taken because the tablets are the size of a thumb. Then the conclusion: "supplements don't work for me," and the whole category gets abandoned. Forms matter: glycinate for magnesium, D3 for vitamin D, a spray or a buffered tablet if swallowing pills is the obstacle. The best product is the one your actual self will take daily.

## 5. The drawer of half-finished bottles

A supplement taken for nine days does roughly nothing. Almost everything on our shelves earns its price over six to twelve weeks of daily use. One bottle, finished, beats five bottles abandoned — which is why we'd rather sell you a single 120-capsule magnesium than three different sleep products. If consistency is your struggle, pick a delivery you can't dodge (a spray by the toothbrush) and a number you can afford to repeat monthly.
