There's a sentence I hear across this counter every single July, almost word for word: *"I'm not sick, Precious. I'm just... flat."* It's said apologetically, as if feeling grey in the greyest month needs a permission slip. It doesn't. July is Mental Illness Awareness Month in South Africa, so let's talk about the winter dip honestly — what it is, what supports it, and where the line is.

## Why the dark gets in

Your brain sets its clock — and a good deal of its chemistry — by light. In a Highveld July you leave for work in the dark, sit under fluorescents all day, and it's dusk again by 17h30. Less bright light hitting your eyes means weaker daily rhythm signals and sluggish serotonin traffic, the chemistry tied closely to steady mood. Stack on winter's other habits — less walking, less sunshine-vitamin, fewer braais and more couch — and the flatness isn't a character flaw. It's arithmetic.

The good news is that arithmetic responds to small, boring inputs.

## What we'd actually suggest

**Light first, because it's free.** Fifteen minutes of morning daylight — coffee on the stoep, parking further from the office door, walking the long way to the taxi rank — does more for the body clock than anything in a capsule. I tell every customer this before I sell them anything, and I mean it. The light has to reach your eyes, so outside beats the brightest office; a grey July morning outdoors still delivers many times more light than indoor bulbs do.

**Keep one moving appointment.** The other winter casualty is movement, and movement is one of the most reliable mood supports we know of — better evidence than most things in bottles, mine included. It doesn't need to be a gym contract in July, of all months. One standing walk with a friend, twice a week, survives winter far better than a private resolution does, because cancelling on a person is harder than cancelling on yourself.

**Saffron, the surprising one.** Of all the botanicals on our shelves, saffron extract has quietly built the best modern research record for supporting a balanced mood. The studies used standardised extracts around 28–30mg a day — which is exactly what a proper product delivers — and showed their effects over four to six weeks, not four days. One capsule daily, judged at the end of the bottle. Important: if you take prescription antidepressants, talk to your doctor before adding saffron alongside them.

**A B-complex for the engine room.** The B vitamins are the spark plugs of nervous-system energy — and a winter diet that's drifted toward white bread and sugar both demands more of them and supplies less. One with breakfast. If your urine turns bright yellow, that's just the riboflavin waving; it's harmless.

**Omega-3 with the emphasis on EPA.** Your brain is mostly fat, and EPA-rich fish oil is the form mood researchers keep coming back to. A concentrated softgel once a day with food covers what a fish-light winter diet doesn't. (If you're already taking our salmon oil for skin or joints, you're covered — don't double up.)

## When NOT to bother

Here's the paragraph I care most about. If the flatness has lasted more than two weeks without lifting, if it's heavier than "flat" — if you've stopped enjoying things you love, you're withdrawing from people, sleep and appetite have changed sharply, or your thoughts have turned dark — that is not a supplement conversation. That's depression territory, and it deserves a professional: your GP, a counsellor, or SADAG (sadag.org), whose helplines are free. A capsule from my shelf is the wrong tool for that job, and I will tell you so to your face, gently, every time. Supplements support an ordinary winter dip. They are not a substitute for help.
