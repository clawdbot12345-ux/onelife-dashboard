A gentleman on semaglutide stood at our Centurion counter in June and told me, almost proudly, that he eats "like a bird now - one small meal a day, maybe two." Then, in the same breath: "But Precious, why am I so *tired*? And my calves cramp at 2am." I didn't need a crystal ball. I needed a calculator.

## The arithmetic nobody does

If you used to eat 2,400 calories and now eat 900, you're not just getting a third of the energy - you're getting roughly a third of the B vitamins, the magnesium, the potassium, the iron, the everything. Your fat stores can cover the missing kilojoules for months. Your nutrient stores are a much shorter book: magnesium and potassium run down in weeks, B12 over months.

Add two GLP-1-specific twists. The medication slows your stomach, which can make you queasy about exactly the foods richest in these nutrients - meat, leafy greens, beans. And many people simply drink less because thirst quietens down alongside hunger. Less fluid plus less sodium, potassium and magnesium is the classic recipe for the 2am calf cramp and the afternoon headache.

## What we'd actually suggest

**B12 and folate first.** These are the workhorses of energy metabolism and the most common genuine gaps when meals shrink - especially if red meat was your main source and it's now the first thing your stomach refuses. One Folate-B12 capsule daily is a small, cheap correction. If capsules sit badly on a queasy stomach, the patch route works around it entirely.

**Electrolytes, daily and boring.** One sachet in a big bottle of water, sipped through the morning. Not the neon sports drinks - the sugar-free kind. Most people feel the difference in headaches and cramps within a week, because the problem was never mysterious.

**Magnesium at night if sleep is scratchy.** Restless legs, light sleep and that wired-tired feeling often travel together with low magnesium when portions shrink. (Our winter sleep guide covers the forms and doses - the short version: glycinate, evening, start low.)

**Eat the nutrient-dense bites first.** When you only have ten bites in you, spend them like a miser: eggs, sardines, liver if you're brave, dark greens. The lettuce can wait.

## The pattern we see at the counter

After a year of GLP-1 customers, the timeline is almost predictable. Weeks one to four: nausea questions. Months two to three: the energy dip and the cramps - that's this article. Months three to five: hair in the drain (next week's guide). It helps to know you're not malfunctioning; you're on a schedule. And it means the fixes work best started early, not once you're flat.

A workable daily rhythm looks like this: electrolyte sachet in your morning water bottle, Folate-B12 with the first food of the day, and your biggest protein serving before noon while appetite is freshest. Magnesium in the evening if sleep is scratchy. That's the whole routine - four small habits, under R25 a day, and it covers the gaps that actually show up in this journey rather than the twenty things the internet is selling this month.

One winter note for Joburg and Pretoria readers: if you were low on vitamin D before the medication (most of us are by July - we've written a whole guide on it), a shrinking diet won't rescue it. It's the one nutrient worth adding to the GP test list alongside B12 and iron.

## When NOT to bother

If you're on a GLP-1 but still eating three balanced meals with meat, dairy and vegetables - your gaps are probably small, and testing beats guessing. Ask your GP for B12, iron and a basic panel before buying anything. And skip the "GLP-1 support gummies" flooding social media: sugar, glitter and a margin. Nothing in this article treats, cures or replaces your medication - it supports the body that's doing the work, and your prescribing doctor should know everything you take.
