Every June it starts. Someone leans on the counter, already sniffing, and asks the same question: *"What must I take so I don't get what everyone at work has?"*

After thirty years of winters behind this counter, here's the honest answer — three products, and just as importantly, two things we routinely tell people to put back on the shelf.

## Why winter actually gets you

It isn't the cold itself. Highveld winters pack us into closed taxis, heated offices and indoor gatherings with the windows shut — more shared air, more shared viruses. At the same time, shorter days mean less sun on your skin, and your vitamin D — a real workhorse of immune function — drifts down right when you need it most. Add the dry winter air drying out the protective lining of your nose and throat, and the maths is simple: more exposure, less defence.

## The stack we hand over

**1. Echinacea — but the right one, started early.** The fresh-plant tinctures are what the better studies actually used, which is why Echinaforce is the bottle our consultants reach for first. The trick nobody tells you: it works best *before* you're sick. Starting it the day your throat already burns is like buying an umbrella in the rain — some help, but you're already wet.

**2. Vitamin C at an honest dose, daily.** Not the fizzy orange sweetie with 80mg hiding behind the flavouring — a proper 1,000mg, every day through winter. Buffered versions matter if your stomach complains; the bioflavonoids in a complex help it do its job. C won't make you bulletproof. What the evidence supports is shorter, milder colds — which, in a Joburg winter, is worth a lot.

**3. Vitamin D, the quiet one.** This is the one most people skip and the one we'd argue hardest for. Office workers see almost no skin-on-sun time from June to August, and low vitamin D is one of the most consistent findings in people who catch everything going around. A daily spray takes three seconds and removes the "I hate swallowing pills" excuse entirely.

## The two things we tell people NOT to buy

Here's the part that surprises people. First: the mega-dose "immune bombs" with twenty ingredients at doses too small to matter. You're paying for the label, not the contents. Second: doubling up — if your daily multivitamin already carries decent zinc and C, adding three more zinc products doesn't build a fortress, it builds expensive urine and an upset stomach. Bring us what you're already taking (or WhatsApp us a photo of the labels) and we'll tell you honestly what's missing — sometimes the answer is "nothing".
