A regular of mine put it perfectly last week: *"Precious, I eat like a saint until the sun goes down. Then I stand in front of the cupboard like it owes me money."* It's July. The cupboard conversations have started. And after thirty years of watching this exact pattern arrive with the cold every year, I can tell you: it's not weak willpower. It's a rollercoaster with a timetable.

## The mechanism behind the 8pm raid

Three things gang up on you in winter. The early dark nudges your brain to chase quick serotonin, and the fastest over-the-counter serotonin lift it knows is refined carbs — hence why nobody craves chicken breast at 20h00. The cold parks you on the couch, so the after-supper walk that used to flatten your evening blood sugar is hibernating. And then the rollercoaster does the rest: white bread or sugar spikes your blood glucose, your body over-corrects, the dip an hour or two later reads as *hunger*, and the cupboard door opens again. Spike, dip, crave, repeat — each round ordering the next.

You don't break a loop like that with willpower. You break it by flattening the spikes.

## What we'd actually suggest

**Psyllium before meals — the unglamorous hero.** A heaped teaspoon stirred briskly into a big glass of water, drunk before lunch and supper. The soluble fibre forms a gel that slows how fast the meal's glucose arrives, which means a gentler rise, a gentler dip, and noticeably less cupboard-magnetism two hours later. It also simply fills you up. Start with one teaspoon a day for a week — psyllium without enough water, or too much too soon, will make itself unpopular — then build up. At about R100 a tub, it's the cheapest effective habit on our shelves.

**Cinnamon and chromium, the steady pair.** Chromium is a trace mineral your body uses in normal insulin function — the very signalling this whole rollercoaster runs on — and cinnamon extract has a long record of supporting healthy glucose metabolism alongside it. One capsule with each main meal, judged after four to six weeks of daily use, not after the weekend.

**Gymnema for the craving hour itself.** This old Ayurvedic herb has a party trick: compounds that sit on your sweet-taste receptors and literally mute them — its Hindi name translates as "sugar destroyer", though "sugar disappointer" is more honest. A capsule taken before *your* danger hour makes the chocolate taste oddly flat, and a flat-tasting chocolate is much easier to put down. It supports the habit change; it doesn't make the decision for you.

And the free fixes that out-punch all three: protein at breakfast (eggs beat toast for evening cravings — the day's first meal sets the rollercoaster's amplitude), supper with actual vegetables in it, and not keeping the slab in the house. You can't raid what isn't there. If the evening still needs a ritual — and in a Joburg July it usually does — make it a warm one that isn't sweet: rooibos, a milky decaf, a small bowl of soup. Half of the 8pm cupboard pull is a body asking for warmth and getting redirected to sugar.

## When NOT to bother

If you have diabetes — or you're on any blood-sugar medication — do not add these on your own. Chromium, cinnamon and gymnema all nudge the same system your medication manages, and the combination is a conversation for your doctor, not a counter purchase. We will always rather send you to the consulting room first. Likewise, if you're sleeping five hours a night, fix that before buying anything: short sleep cranks hunger hormones hard enough that no capsule can shout over it. And if the night eating feels compulsive or distressed rather than just habitual, that's worth raising with a professional — honestly and without shame.
